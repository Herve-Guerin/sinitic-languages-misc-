Extended Pinyin Scheme for the learning of dialectal pronunciations of Chinese characters with its associated tables

 

 

Objectives

 

The present Extended Pinyin Scheme (XPS) as well as the set of tables displayed here are aimed at all persons willing to learn one or several Chinese dialects, assuming that they already know some Standard Modern Mandarin (SMM, aka Putonghua).

 

Main objectives are:

- Easing the memorization of most current Chinese characters in any Chinese dialect,

- Making it possible to get a more general knowledge of overall Chinese phonology that what can be obtained from the knowledge of any single dialect.

 

This is done with help of the present XPS which is devised to give a phonetic-based orthography of characters from which to derive actual pronunciations in modern dialects, while:

- Taking into account most of the phonetic distinctions in the major modern dialects,

- Avoiding useless historical distinctions when considering those dialects,

 

This work is not:

-          a new interdialectal Romanization to be used as a writing system, as exemplified in Zhao Yuanren’s (赵元任) work ‘A project for General Chinese’ (通字方案).

-          a rhyme book with characters classified according to their Early or Late Middle Chinese (EMC, c. 5th-7th century AD; LMC, c. 10th-12th century AD) pronunciation, along the lines of Qieyun (切韵), Guangyun (广韵) or the ‘Questionnaire of characters for dialect survey’ (方言调查字表 ).

 

Overall layout

 

Nevertheless the overall structure found in those works has been retained, namely a general layout proceeding with:

o       finals (rhymes, 韵母) defining pages,

o       medials (介音) defining big groups inside pages,

o       initials (声母) on distinct lines inside big groups,

o       tones (声调) displayed on separate columns.

 

There are in a page three big zones, from left to right:

-          the XPS values of the characters, organized into 3 columns according to the Middle Chinese tones (level (平) / entering (入), rising (上), departing (去)),

-          the Chinese characters themselves, organized in the same 3 columns,

-          the pronunciation of those characters in the modern dialects, also in the same three columns (plus a column for listing non regular pronunciations), with one tab for each dialect.

 

Extended Pinyin Scheme

 

The Extended Pinyin Scheme (XPS) designed here is based on the pinyin used to transcript SMM/Putonghua, which has been further extended in order to provide for the main phonetic distinctions made by major modern dialects. Pinyin is the only phonetic transcription known by everybody, native Chinese (especially through the use of Input Method Editors) as well as foreigners studying Putonghua, since its use as the basis of XPS.

 

Major consequences of this choice are:

 

-          Tones are still marked by diacritics and not numbers. In addition to Putonghua’s 1st tone (ā, dark level, 阴平), 2nd tone (á, light level, 阳平), 3rd tone (ǎ, rising, 上声), 4th tone (à, departing, 去声), we also use ã (for finals considered as dark rising, 阳上, ǎ being then maintained for light rising, 阴上) and â (for finals considered as dark departing, 阳去, à being maintained for light departing, 阴去). For those syllables with entering tone 入声, we mark the distinction between light departing tone, 阴入(no tone marking) and dark departing â (same marking as dark departing tone阳去).

 

-          Original Middle Chinese voiced consonants are not written using special symbols; instead we reproduce the overall schema found in Putonghua and its pinyin orthography. Namely the symbols for non aspirated consonants (b, d, z, zh, j, g) are used before finals now in the dark rising, departing and entering tones, while the symbols for aspirated consonants (p, t, c, ch, q, k) are used before finals now in the dark level tone.

 

-          We maintain the set of consonants (j, q, x) which come from both Middle Chinese fronted dental affricates (z, c, s) and velars (g, k, h). We use (z, c, s) for the former set, and (j, q, x) for the latter (except with less fronted medials (original 2nd grade, 二等), for which we use (g, k, h)).

 

-          With nasal and liquid initials, dark rising and departing tones are noted with symbols for level rising and departing tones in order to keep closer from pinyin. For instance mǎ and not mã, mà and not mâ.

 

-          Ü is maintained as a symbol in lü, nü, lüe, nüe. In addition to keep the system closer to pinyin, it enables keeping the distinction with nyu (coming from fronted LMC ŋ (疑)).

 

-          Some abbreviations in pinyin, such as –ui (for –uei) and –iu (for –iou) are kept, which precludes the use of those letter combinations in a more natural matter.  Especially, the rounded fronted finals (撮合口) are rendered by the combination –yu (or –ü before n and l) instead of –iu.

 

 

 

Assignment of phonetic orthography to characters

 

The assignment is done according to distinctions made amongst modern dialects. If totally exhaustive, we should decide that if any dialect makes a difference between any couple of characters, this distinction must be taken into account in the extended pinyin. In fact distinctions are made only if some regularity is observed in those differences.

 

The best starting point to establish the needed distinctions is the set of categories found in the Late Middle Chinese phonology; those categories are a little bit too much fine-grained with respect to modern pronunciations, so some lumping have been introduced in them. Practically we have started from Zhao Yuanren’s distinctions, making some additional lumping as well as splitting.

 

We have used for those lumping and splitting the ‘Dictionnary of pronunciation of characters in Chinese dialects’ (汉语方音字汇), which lists the pronunciations of about 3000 characters from the following dialects : Beijing, Jinan, Xi’an, Taiyuan, Wuhan, Chengdu, Hefei, Yangzhou, Suzhou, Wenzhou, Changsha, Shuangfeng, Nanchang, Meixian, Guangzhou, Yangjiang, Xiamen, Chaozhou, Fuzhou, Jian’ou.

We have not taken in account Sinoxenic pronunciations (Sino-Japanese, Sino-Korean, Sino-Vietnamese).

 

See the annexe for more details on those lumping and splitting.

 

It happens that some characters have in most modern dialects an irregular pronunciation according to the rules deriving modern pronunciations from ancient LMC to modern ones. In that case, the irregular pronunciation becomes the reference, and the extended pinyin reflects that state of things. In some cases, variants are to be found equally distributed among dialects; we base then the orthography on Mandarin, considering also Wu and Yue dialects. In some other cases, the SMM pronunciation is itself clearly irregular while that of most other dialects is regular; the orthography reflects then the pronunciations of those other dialects.

 

 

Extended Pinyin (detailed)

 

Any page is defined by a set of finals according to SMM (Standard Modern Mandarin, Putonghua). For instance, the first page groups together the characters whose finals are pinyin –A, -IA or UA. In a page, the lines are sorted into big groups according to the order of those finals in Putonghua, in the example, first –A, then –IA and finally –UA. This order follows generally that of the medials, i.e. Ø, -i-, -u-, -yu-.

 

In such groups defined by the combination of final and medial, the lines of characters are then classified according to the grouping of the values of their initial in Putonghua:

-          Labials: B, P, M, F

-          Alveolars: D, T, N, L

-          Sibilant Alveolars: Z, C, S

-          Retroflexes: ZH, CH, SH, R

-          Alveolo-palatals: J, Q, X

-          Velars: G, K, H

-          Non-consonant: (Ø), Y, W

 

For instance, with the page –A, -IA, -UA, we have following groupings:

For –A:

-          BA, PA, MA, FA

-          DA, TA, NA, LA

-          ZA, CA, SA

-          ZHA, CHA, SHA

-          GA, KA, HA

-          A

For –IA:

-          JIA, QIA, XIA

-          YA

For –UA:

-          ZHUA, SHUA

-          GUA, KUA, HUA

-          WA

 

Those first grouping done along the lines of Standard Modern Mandarin, and posited on column 1, the characters are then grouped according to the present Extended Pinyin Scheme. Basically, we define sub-groups according first to the initials in XPS and then to the finals.

 

For instance, inside pinyin groups ZHA, CHA, SHA, we have following extended pinyin sub-groups:

-          DZHA, TCHA

-          DZHAP

-          ZHA, CHA, SHA

-          ZHAP, CHAP, SHAP

-          ZHAT, CHAT, SHAT

The finals are sorted first by their codas (for instance Ø vs –P, -T, -K, or –M vs –N) and then by their nuclei (main vowel).

 

Inside these smaller groups, each line corresponds to one LMC initial, in the following order (giving first LMC pronunciation then reflexes in extended pinyin, depending on the evolution of initials depending on the LMC values for tones, as well as on palatalization effects):

 - 帮 p(B), 滂 pʰ(P), 并 b(P, B), 明 m(M), 非敷 f, fʰ (F), 奉 v (F), 微ɱ (V)

- 端 t(D), 透 tʰ(T), 定 d(T, D), 泥娘 n, ɳ (N), 来 l(L)

- 精 ts(Z), 清 tsʰ(C), 从 dz(C, Z), 心 s(S), 邪 z(Z)

- 知 ʈ(DZH), 彻 ʈʰ(TCH), 澄 ɖ(TCH, DZH)

- 照 tʂ(ZH), 穿 tʂʰ(CH), 床 dʐ(CH, ZH), 审 ʂ(SH), 禅 ʐ(SH), 日ɻ(R)

- 见 k(G, J), 溪 kʰ(K, Q), 群 ɡ(K, Q, G, J), 疑 ŋ(NG, NY), 晓 x(H, X), 匣 ɣ(H, X)

- 影 ʔ((Ø), Y, W), 喻 j(Y, W)

 

Each column corresponds to one LMC tone (level 平, rising 上, departing 去), unless the final (with a –P, -T or –K coda) corresponds to an entering入 tone, in which case this tone is the only possible value, in first column. Combining the voicing of initials and LMC tone values, we come in extended pinyin to the following tone values and notations:

-          dark level (阴平): ā

-          light level (阳平): á

-          dark rising (阴上): ǎ

-          light rising (阳上): ã

-          dark departing (阴去): à

-          light departing (阳去): â

-          dark entering (阴入): a

-          light entering (阳入): â

Chinese Characters and Glosses

 

A little bit more than 3000 characters have been included in the tables, that is those characters listed in the ‘Dictionnary of pronunciation of characters in Chinese dialects’ 汉语方音字汇, plus some sufficiently frequent additional characters found in Zhao Yuanren’s  ‘A project for General Chinese’, 通字方案.

 

Many characters are the written forms of different morphemes with different meanings and/or sounds (in LMC, SMM or other modern dialects). The corresponding entries are distinguished with glosses in smaller font (taken from either汉语方音字汇, 通字方案, or 方言调查字表).

For instance: guōn 冠衣～, guòn 冠～军.

 

Numbers in glosses refer to tones in LMC (1 平, 2 上, 3 去, 4 入).

For instance: yî 易难～, yîk 易4交～.

 

Letters v. and n. stand for (as a) verb and noun.

For instance: zuān 钻1v., zuàn 钻n.

 

Simplified characters have been used instead of traditional ones. Many simplified characters transcribe different morphemes originally written with different (traditional) characters; those morphemes are differentiated using the traditional forms.

For instance: tchǒu丑=醜, tchǒu, 丑 / huî 汇=彙, huê 汇～合.

Dialectal Pronunciations

 

For the time being, we have included only Shanghainese and Cantonese in our tables. Taiwanese as the third most influential dialect should be the next to be provided for.

 

For most dialects, including Shanghainese and Cantonese, the phonetic values are displayed on 4 columns. Taiwanese, considering the pervasiveness of double readings, will need another layout.

 

The first three columns give regular dialectal pronunciations, parallelizing the level/entering, rising and departing tone columns of XPS and characters columns.

The fourth column presents pronunciations differing from regular pronunciations, i.e. from what should be expected according to rules deriving dialectal pronunciations from XP notations (and behind that from LMC pronunciations for most characters). This column gives also additional information in case of different literary and colloquial readings (文白异读).

 

The dialectal pronunciations are given using either the IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet) or an existing standard which has achieved official or broad recognition, if any.

For Shanghainese, such a standard has unfortunately not gained pre-eminence yet, even First or Second Pinyin Schemes for Shanghainese, 上海话拼音方案第一式 or 二式, so IPA is used.

The phonetic readings have been found mainly in “a Chinese-English Shanghai Dialect Dictionary”, 汉英上海口语词典 complemented with “Shanghainese Sound Files”, 上海话音档.

For Cantonese, we use jyutping, which is the de facto standard.

We don’t describe here IPA or jyutping, please refer to sources such as Wikipedia in English or Chinese.

 

When there is a systematic difference between literary and colloquial readings, the literary reading is given in blue font, the colloquial readings in grey font.

 

For instance, in Cantonese:

It is not the case that all characters in such a row have both literal and colloquial readings. Some might have only one of those two readings. The 4th column presents which characters have which readings.

 

For instance:

佳稼 are only found with the literary reading (文),假贾 with the colloquial reading (白), and家加嘉架驾嫁价 with both (文白).

 

Many characters have both readings without any systematic rule working behind; the fourth column contains then the transcription of both readings. For instance, in Shanghainese:

Other exceptions are transcribed with the irregular phonemes written in red.

For instance, in Shanghainese: 打: taŋ34. By the way, this pronunciation is in phase with that of LMC, but not with that of the majority of dialects.

Two readings, one regular, the other not, can also be found.

For instance, in Cantonese: 拉: laai1,laa1

 

Another feature is the absence of a definite regular rule for a given dialect, absence which gives way to characters in a same row having different pronunciations without being able to mark this difference in the XP scheme.

The two readings are then separated by /, and the distribution of characters according to those readings is given in the 4th column.

4) A note on LMC dark rising tones (阳上) rendering in present XP scheme:

 

Only two major dialects, Yue and Chaozhou dialects have maintained a distinct阳上tonal category. In other dialects, this has merged into (阳)去, (dark) departing. It appears that among original LMC阳上 characters, a big part of it have also become阳去 in Yue dialects, whereas in Chaozhou dialects, not only have all LMC 阳上 characters been kept as such, but also some 阳去 characters have become 阳上 as well.

 

We have chosen to align Extended Pinyin with Yue dialects, because Cantonese has a far bigger importance than Chaozhou dialect.

For information, we present here a list of original阳上 characters which have become阳去 in Cantonese:
